{% raw %}Ask This Old House heating and plumbing expert Richard Trethewey removes an old bathtub spout and installs an overflow for safety. (See below for a shopping list, tools, and steps.)<br /><br />SUBSCRIBE to This Old House: <a rel="nofollow" target="blank" href="http://bit.ly/SubscribeThisOldHouse">http://bit.ly/SubscribeThisOldHouse</a><br /><br />Cost: $60—$100 for parts<br />Time: 3 to 4 hours<br />Difficulty: Hard<br /><br />Shopping List for How to Add an Overflow to a Bathtub: <br />- Bath waste and overflow toe-touch kit<br />- 1 1/2-inch PVC piping fittings to design<br />- 1 1/2-inch PVC pipe p-trap<br />- Plumber’s putty<br />- PVC primer and pipe cement<br /><br />Tools for How to Add an Overflow to a Bathtub: <br />- Basin wrench<br />- Reciprocating saw<br />- Open-end wrench<br /><br />Steps for How to Add an Overflow to a Bathtub: <br />1. Turn off water supply to bathtub. <br />2. Remove old spout by unscrewing it from the rear space of the bathtub with a basin wrench. <br />3. Remove line to old drain shoe with a reciprocating saw and unscrew the shoe by hand. <br />4. Add plumber’s putty to the toe-touch and place it in the empty drain hole. <br />5. Have a friend hold the shoe on the outside end while you screw and tighten the toe-touch into the shoe with an open-end wrench.<br />6. Have a friend place the back of the overflow along with a gasket through the upper hole in the bathtub. <br />7. Now tighten up the overflow with the nut from the overflow toe-touch kit. <br />8. Pop on the cover to the overflow on the bathtub side. <br />9. Depending on design of piping, prefit PVC pipes to your custom system. <br />10. When you find a design that fits, make the connections with PVC primer and pipe cement.<br /><br />Resources:<br />To replace the old spout with an overflow, Richard used an ABA Classic Bath Waste and Overflow Touch Toe made by AB&amp;A [<a rel="nofollow" target="blank" href="https://ipscorp.com/plumbing/brands/aba/].">https://ipscorp.com/plumbing/brands/aba/].</a><br /><br />Richard also used standard 1½-inch PVC pipe and fittings, which can be found at home centers and plumbing supply stores.<br /><br />About Ask This Old House TV:<br />Homeowners have a virtual truckload of questions for us on smaller projects, and we're ready to answer.
