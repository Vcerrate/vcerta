{% raw %}As soon as they hear that I’m Yolngu and that I can speak language, it’s almost like a tension in the patient drops and they just relax,” Dr Mangatjay McGregor.<br /><br />Mangatjay McGregor is starring in an extraordinary scene on the fifth floor of the Royal Darwin Hospital.<br /><br />The first-year doctor is talking to the family of eight-year-old patient Tyson Pascoe, who lies in bed grinning, hoodie up and eating a biscuit.<br /><br />The setting would be routine, particularly in a hospital, if not for one defining element: all are speaking Yolngu-Matha, the major dialect used among Yolngu people from north-east Arnhem land.<br /><br />Seven months ago, Dr McGregor swapped his scrubs and stethoscope for graduation robes and a mortarboard, and walked on stage at the Adelaide Convention Centre.<br /><br />Cheers erupted in the back row, and later others said his family was the loudest of all who attended the Flinders University graduation ceremony.<br /><br />While the 26-year-old could hear their yells, he said it took a few more weeks for the weight of the moment to register.
